"Post more" is the
worst advice.

Content·5 min read·Hamed Ashouri

"Just post more" is the marketing advice you'll hear most this year — and the one most likely to waste your time. Volume feels like progress. It almost never is.

Posting more only works if the thing you're posting is already clear. Add volume to a muddy message and you don't get reach — you get more of the same confusion, more often. The problem was never that you posted too little. It's that nothing you posted pointed in one direction.

More posting isn't a strategy

A full calendar feels productive, but activity isn't direction. Ten posts that each say something slightly different don't add up — they cancel out. Your audience never hears one idea long enough to remember it. You've been busy, and busy is not the same as clear.

Why volume backfires

Three quiet costs pile up when "more" becomes the plan:

  • Your messages compete with each other. Attention is finite; scattered themes split it instead of stacking it.
  • The algorithm rewards resonance, not frequency. One post people save and share beats ten they scroll past.
  • You burn out. The treadmill is exhausting, and exhaustion is where good businesses quietly abandon marketing.
Ten scattered messages don't add up to one clear one. Repetition makes you memorable — variety just makes you forgettable.

The fix: one message, many forms

Pick the single idea you want to be known for. Then say it ten different ways — a story, a myth-vs-truth, a client example, a short video, a carousel — instead of ten different ideas said once each. Same message, new angle, every time. That's not repetitive to your audience; it's how they finally learn what you stand for.

What to do this week

  • Name your one message. The sentence you want to own in your market.
  • Plan your next ten posts as variations on it — different formats, same core idea.
  • Measure saves and replies, not just reach. Those are the signals that a message is landing.

Do that, and you'll post less, mean more, and finally see the work compound. Clarity repeated beats volume scattered — every single time.
